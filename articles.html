Hardware Safety Protocol // 01

Why You Should NEVER Put a Wet Phone in Rice

For decades, the "bag of rice" trick has been the go-to advice for water-damaged electronics. However, in 2024, Apple and Samsung officially updated their support guidelines to warn against this practice. Why? Because it does more harm than good.

The Starch Dust Problem

Uncooked rice is covered in microscopic starch dust. When you place a wet phone into a bag of rice, this dust mixes with the moisture to create a thick, glue-like paste. This paste can lodge itself into:

  • The charging port (preventing cable connection).
  • The speaker grill (permanently muffling audio).
  • The microphone array (ruining call quality).

The Better Alternative: Airflow & Frequency

Instead of absorbents, modern smartphones need airflow and ejection. Gravity is your best friend. Place the phone upright with the ports facing down. Then, use a 165Hz Ejection Tone. This frequency vibrates the water molecules violently enough to break their surface tension, allowing them to drip out naturally without introducing foreign contaminants like rice dust.

Wireless Audio // 02

How to Eject Water from AirPods and Galaxy Buds

Wireless earbuds are notoriously difficult to clean because they lack large batteries to drive powerful bass vibrations. However, they are highly susceptible to sweat and rain damage during workouts.

The "Centrifugal" Method

Since AirPods have small drivers, acoustic ejection needs physical help. We recommend a hybrid approach:

  1. Step 1: Connect your earbuds to this device via Bluetooth.
  2. Step 2: Set our tool to "High Pitch / 1200Hz" (available in the Dust Mode). High frequencies travel better through the smaller mesh of earbuds.
  3. Step 3: While the sound plays, hold the earbud by the stem and gently shake it downward. The vibration loosens the water, and the centrifugal force of the shake expels it.

Warning: Charging Cases

Never put wet earbuds back into their charging case. The case usually has zero water resistance. Even a drop of sweat on the charging contacts can corrode the copper pins, destroying the case's ability to charge.

Technical Specs // 03

Understanding IP67 vs. IP68: Is Your Phone Actually Waterproof?

Marketing terms like "Waterproof" are misleading. In the engineering world, we use the Ingress Protection (IP) scale. Knowing your rating is critical to knowing if your device can survive a swim.

The First Digit: Dust

Almost all modern flagships are "IP6x". The '6' means they are dust-tight. However, as we discuss in our "Dust Integrity" section, this seal degrades over time as the adhesive ages.

The Second Digit: Water

  • IPx7 (iPhone XR, Older Pixels): Submersion up to 1 meter for 30 minutes. Good for a toilet drop or rain, but bad for swimming pools (pressure).
  • IPx8 (iPhone 15, S24 Ultra): Submersion beyond 1 meter (usually 1.5m to 6m) for 30 minutes. These can survive a shallow dive.

The "Speaker Trap"

Even with IP68, the speaker must have a hole to let sound out. Water can enter this hole but is stopped by a mesh barrier.
