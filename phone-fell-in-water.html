EMERGENCY: PHONE IN WATER
Every second counts. Learn the immediate triage steps to prevent a fatal short-circuit, protect your motherboard, and safely eject liquid from your device.
Topic Index / Troubleshooting / Phone Fell In Water

It happens in slow motion: your phone slips from your hand and plunges into a sink, a swimming pool, or a puddle. The immediate aftermath is pure panic. You retrieve it, and while the screen might still be glowing, the underlying threat of liquid damage to the motherboard and the acoustic hardware is actively ticking down.

If your phone just fell in water, stop and read this guide carefully. What you do in the next five minutes will determine whether your phone survives unscathed or becomes an expensive paperweight. Do not plug it in, do not shake it violently, and do not reach for a bag of rice. Follow this emergency triage protocol instead.

Is Your Phone Out of the Water?

If the device is already wiped down and you are ready to clear the speakers, initiate the acoustic ejection cycle immediately.

Phase 1: Immediate Triage and the "Do Nots"

Your phone is out of the water. The priority right now is preventing a catastrophic electrical short-circuit. The water itself rarely kills a phone; electricity traveling through the water kills the phone.

CRITICAL RULE 1: Do NOT plug the phone in. If you insert a charging cable into a wet port, you will instantly send electrical current across wet pins. This causes immediate corrosion, sparks, and can permanently fry the power management IC chip on your motherboard. If your phone was plugged in when it fell, unplug it from the wall outlet first before touching the phone, if safe to do so.

  • Do NOT shake the phone violently: While it seems logical to "shake the water out," violent shaking forces water that was sitting harmlessly on the outside of the internal seals deeper into the chassis, potentially bypassing the IP68 protection.
  • Do NOT press any physical buttons: Pressing the volume or power buttons can squeeze tiny droplets of water past the rubber button gaskets. If the phone is on, leave it on. If it turned off, do not try to turn it back on yet.
  • Do NOT use a hair dryer or oven: Direct, intense heat will melt the specialized adhesives that hold your phone's screen and back glass together, completely ruining its structural integrity and destroying its battery.

Phase 2: Assessing the Liquid Type

Not all liquids are created equal. The type of liquid your phone fell into drastically changes your recovery strategy. Smartphone IP68 water resistance ratings are tested exclusively in controlled, static, pure freshwater.

The Freshwater Drop (Sink, Puddle, Rain)

If your phone fell into relatively clean freshwater, you are in the best possible scenario. The internal seals are highly effective against this. Your primary concern is simply removing the water trapped in the speaker grills and charging port.

The Corrosive Drop (Saltwater, Ocean)

Dropping your phone in the ocean is a massive emergency. Saltwater is highly conductive and incredibly corrosive. If saltwater is allowed to dry inside your charging port or speaker grills, it will leave behind salt crystals that rapidly eat through the metallic components.

Immediate Action: Paradoxically, if your phone falls in saltwater, you must immediately rinse the bottom ports with clean, fresh tap water. You must wash the corrosive salt away before you attempt to dry the phone.

The Surfactant Drop (Soapy Water, Dishwater)

Soaps and detergents contain chemicals called surfactants. These chemicals purposefully lower the surface tension of water. This means soapy water can slip right past the rubber gaskets and waterproof meshes of your phone much easier than regular water. Like saltwater, rinse the phone gently with fresh water to remove the soap.

Phase 3: The Safe Drying Protocol

Now that the phone is safe from electrical shorts and corrosive chemicals, we must execute the drying phase.

Step 1: The Exterior Wipe

Remove the phone from its protective case. Take a clean, dry microfiber cloth (or a highly absorbent cotton towel) and gently dab the entire exterior of the phone dry. Pay special attention to the charging port, the bottom speaker grill, and the top earpiece. Dab the liquid, do not scrub it.

Step 2: The Acoustic Ejection (Speaker Cleaner Pro)

At this stage, your phone is dry on the outside, but water is trapped inside the microscopic holes of your speaker mesh via capillary action. Your speakers will sound heavily muffled.

You must use kinetic sound waves to push this water out. Use your phone's hardware buttons to turn the media volume to 100%. Hold the phone vertically so the charging port faces the ground. Navigate to the Speaker Cleaner Pro tool on our homepage and run the Pro Mode Auto-Cycle. You will hear a loud, oscillating tone, and you will likely see droplets of water being physically punched out of the bottom of the phone.

Step 3: The Wait (And the Truth About Rice)

Once you have run the acoustic ejection tool two or three times, the heavy water is gone. However, a microscopic film of humidity remains. You must allow this to evaporate natively.

The Final Nail in the Rice Myth: The internet will tell you to bury the phone in a bowl of uncooked rice. Do not do this. Rice is too slow to act as a desiccant, and worse, it is covered in starch dust. This dust gets into your wet charging port and speakers, turning into a hardened cement paste that will ruin your device forever. Apple and Samsung both officially warn against using rice.

Instead of rice, place the phone in a dry, well-ventilated area. If you have a fan, you can point a gentle breeze (cool air only, no heat) at the phone. If you happen to have Silica Gel Packets (the little "Do Not Eat" packets that come in shoe boxes), placing the phone in an airtight container with several of these packets is a highly effective, safe drying method.

Phase 4: When is it Safe to Charge?

The most important question is when you can plug the phone back in. If you use an iPhone or a modern Samsung Galaxy, the operating system will likely display a "Liquid Detected in Connector" warning if you attempt to charge it. Trust this warning.

As a general rule, you should wait a minimum of 5 to 6 hours before attempting to plug a cable into a phone that was fully submerged, even if it seems dry. If you absolutely must charge the phone during this window, rely exclusively on a wireless Qi charger or MagSafe puck, as these do not rely on exposed metallic pins to transfer power.

Conclusion

Dropping a phone in water is stressful, but by acting rationally and understanding the physics of liquid ingress, you can save your device. Avoid chargers, avoid heat, wash away salt, and rely on targeted acoustic frequencies to clear your hardware. Keep this protocol in mind, and your device will survive to see another day.
